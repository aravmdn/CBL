# Design Principles, AI Design, and PowerPoint Application

Date: 2026-05-11

## Research Scope

This report synthesizes research-backed design principles, common visual-design failures, what makes designs memorable and appealing, how those ideas apply to PowerPoint decks, and how AI can support the workflow. It focuses on practical use for student/project presentations rather than corporate slideware.

## Executive Takeaways

1. Strong design is mostly controlled attention: the audience should know what to look at first, second, and last.
2. The best slides reduce cognitive load: fewer competing elements, clear grouping, nearby labels, and no decorative noise.
3. Memorable slides usually contain one dominant idea plus one distinctive visual treatment, not many medium-sized things.
4. Animation works when it explains sequence, causality, emphasis, or state change; it becomes harmful when it merely decorates.
5. AI is useful for ideation, rewriting, layout variation, image generation, and consistency checks, but still needs human art direction and QA.
6. For PowerPoints, the winning pattern is not “make every slide fancy.” It is: one slide, one job, one dominant read, one controlled visual system.

## Core Design Principles

### 1. Visual Hierarchy

Hierarchy answers: “What should I look at first?” Use size, weight, contrast, position, and spacing to create order. In slides, the hierarchy should usually be:

- Main claim or title
- Key visual/evidence object
- Short supporting label or proof
- Footer/source/context

Practical rule: if the audience cannot identify the main point in 3 seconds, the slide has too many competing priorities.

### 2. Scale

Scale is not just “make the title big.” It is a ranking system. Use no more than a few meaningful size levels:

- Hero title or key phrase
- Section/subheading
- Body/callout
- Caption/source

Bad pattern: everything is 18-28 pt, so nothing feels important.

### 3. Contrast

Contrast creates distinction. It can be color, size, shape, weight, density, texture, or motion. Strong contrast makes slides feel intentional.

Good contrast:

- Large claim against quiet background
- Bright accent used only for key terms
- One high-energy image against restrained text
- Muted support text around a strong central visual

Bad contrast:

- Low-contrast gray text on dark backgrounds
- Many saturated colors with no hierarchy
- Random accent colors with no semantic meaning

### 4. Balance

Balance means visual weight feels deliberate, not necessarily symmetrical. A large title on one side can be balanced by an image, chart, or dense object on the other.

Good slide balance often uses:

- A dominant object placed off-center
- Whitespace as an active design element
- One strong visual field balanced by compact text

### 5. Gestalt Grouping

Gestalt principles explain how viewers group elements. For slides, the most useful are:

- Proximity: things close together feel related.
- Similarity: things that look alike feel part of the same system.
- Continuity: the eye follows lines, paths, and aligned edges.
- Closure: the viewer completes implied forms.
- Common fate: things moving or changing together feel connected.

PowerPoint application: group related text and visuals physically close; avoid putting a label far away from the thing it describes.

### 6. Cognitive Load

Cognitive load is the mental work required to process information. Slides fail when the audience must read, listen, and decode layout all at once.

Reduce load by:

- Removing nonessential text and visuals
- Splitting dense ideas across multiple slides
- Placing labels next to visuals
- Using consistent visual grammar
- Revealing information in sequence when needed

### 7. Multimedia Learning

For explanatory slides, Mayer’s principles translate directly into presentation design:

- Coherence: remove irrelevant words, images, sounds, and effects.
- Signaling: highlight structure with emphasis, labels, color, or animation.
- Redundancy: avoid reading large blocks that are also on screen.
- Spatial contiguity: keep labels close to what they label.
- Temporal contiguity: show narration/animation together when explaining a process.
- Segmenting: break a process into steps.

## Major Design Flaws

### Text Problems

- Paragraphs on slides
- Bullet lists with equal visual weight
- Long titles that wrap awkwardly
- Tiny captions required for understanding
- Copy that describes the slide instead of making a claim

Fix:

- Rewrite body copy into claims, labels, or speaker notes.
- One sentence per idea.
- Use a slide title as a conclusion, not a topic label.

### Layout Problems

- Everything centered by default
- No clear margins or alignment
- Objects floating without relationship
- Repeated “title + three boxes” layouts
- Visible boxes used to make weak content look designed

Fix:

- Use a stable grid.
- Align edges.
- Create obvious groups.
- Use open typography and visual objects before adding boxes.

### Visual Problems

- Decorative images that do not add meaning
- Stretched or badly cropped images
- Generic icons used as filler
- Too many colors
- Stock templates that do not match the topic

Fix:

- Use images as evidence, atmosphere, metaphor, or subject matter.
- Use 2-3 primary colors plus neutrals.
- Repeat a motif intentionally.

### Animation Problems

- Every bullet flies in
- Random transitions between slides
- Slow effects that delay the speaker
- Motion that does not explain anything
- Too many object animations in online presenting

Fix:

- Use animation for sequence, emphasis, transformation, or suspense.
- Prefer fades, wipes, and simple builds.
- Keep one transition type across most of the deck.

### Content Problems

- Slides answer “what did we do?” but not “why does it matter?”
- No narrative arc
- No tension or decision
- All slides have the same emotional energy

Fix:

- Build a story: setup, tension, mechanism, evidence, risk, next step.
- Give each slide one job.
- Vary density: some slides explain, some land a point.

## What Makes Design Memorable

### Distinctiveness

Memorable design usually has one unusual, specific element. This connects to the isolation/Von Restorff effect: an element that differs from a set is more likely to be noticed and remembered.

Slide application:

- One oversized word
- One striking crop
- One unusual comparison
- One strong color accent
- One moment of silence/whitespace

### Picture Superiority

Research on the picture-superiority effect generally finds that pictures are often remembered better than words. For slides, this does not mean “add more images.” It means use one meaningful visual rather than many decorative visuals.

Slide application:

- Replace a paragraph with a diagram.
- Replace a process list with a flow.
- Replace abstract claims with concrete imagery.

### Story Structure

People remember movement: before/after, problem/response, input/process/output, risk/mitigation, question/reveal.

Slide application:

- Make the deck feel like a sequence, not a folder of pages.
- Use recurring motifs to show progression.
- Use section slides or visual shifts at turning points.

### Emotional Fit

Design should feel like the subject. A “Living Art” deck should not look like a finance dashboard. The visual system should express pulse, signal, camera, body, sound, and atmosphere.

## Applying This to PowerPoint

### Slide-Level Checklist

For every slide, ask:

1. What is the one job of this slide?
2. What should the audience notice first?
3. What can be removed?
4. Is the main point visible from the back of a room?
5. Are related text and visuals close together?
6. Is the slide useful without me reading it aloud?
7. Does animation reveal structure or just decorate?

### Deck-Level Checklist

Across the whole deck:

1. Does the cover feel specific to this topic?
2. Is there a consistent color/type/motif system?
3. Are slide layouts varied but coherent?
4. Are dense slides balanced with sparse slides?
5. Does the ending land somewhere, or just stop?
6. Are transitions consistent?
7. Is the deck editable, not a pile of screenshots?

### Recommended PowerPoint Pattern

Use this structure for concept/project decks:

1. Cover: memorable promise or visual concept
2. Direction: what the idea is
3. System: input/process/output
4. Challenge: what is hard
5. Evidence: what you already know or tested
6. Build plan: timeline and roles
7. Risks: what could fail
8. Closing: what should happen next

### Animation Strategy

Use animation only where it helps:

- Input/process/output: reveal one stage at a time.
- Timeline: reveal weeks sequentially.
- Risk slide: reveal risk after context.
- Visual explanation: use fades or wipes to guide attention.

Avoid:

- By-letter text
- Busy transitions
- Spinning/zooming effects
- Animations on every object

## How AI Can Help

### Strong Uses of AI

AI can:

- Rewrite paragraphs into sharp slide claims.
- Generate multiple layout directions.
- Suggest narrative arcs.
- Create visual metaphors.
- Generate or edit imagery.
- Produce consistent slide systems.
- Check text density and hierarchy.
- Convert notes into speaker notes.
- Draft alt text and accessibility checks.
- Produce native editable PowerPoint when using the right tools.

### Weak Uses of AI

AI still struggles with:

- Knowing the true intent behind a project.
- Avoiding generic “pretty template” design.
- Maintaining precise editability across complex layouts.
- Choosing images that are both factual and tasteful.
- Fine typography, spacing, and contrast QA.
- Native animation reasoning.
- Knowing what should be removed.

### Best AI Workflow for Deck Design

1. Human gives source content and audience.
2. AI extracts the story and reduces text.
3. AI proposes 2-3 art directions.
4. Human chooses the emotional direction.
5. AI builds a native editable draft.
6. AI renders previews and checks layout.
7. Human reviews for meaning and taste.
8. AI patches details and adds restrained animation.

## Practical Rubric for Future Decks

Score each slide 0-2:

| Criterion | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| One clear job | unclear | partly clear | instantly clear |
| Hierarchy | flat | acceptable | strong first/second/third read |
| Text economy | too much | manageable | concise |
| Visual relevance | decorative | somewhat useful | essential |
| Memorability | generic | competent | distinctive |
| Animation | distracting/none where needed | acceptable | reinforces meaning |
| Accessibility | poor contrast/tiny text | mostly readable | robust |

Target: most slides should score 10+ out of 14.
